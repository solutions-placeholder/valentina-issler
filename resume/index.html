Career Advice

Top 3 Tips

  • Apply, apply, apply — You miss 100% of the shots you don’t take. Don’t pre-reject yourself by worrying about funding or logistics.
  • Be yourself — Authenticity will help you find the right match. You don’t want to perform every day at work.
  • Rejection is redirection — If it didn’t work out, it wasn’t aligned. Better to know now than be stuck later.

Why am I giving this advice?

Many intelligent, bright people don’t land opportunities due to a poor understanding of what a personal statement really needs to do. I’ve lived in Brazil, Spain, the UK, and Belgium — and I’ve seen how different the education and hiring processes are. For instance, Brazil focuses heavily on grades; in the UK, once you hit the grade threshold, your personal statement becomes the differentiator.

I’ve helped over 50 people refine their personal statements. Not all were successful — some got in, some didn’t — but I’ve learned what works and what doesn’t. Successful ones led to:

  • Offers at top UK universities
  • Master’s programs in the US (including UC Berkeley)
  • Training contracts at leading law firms
  • Graduate jobs at competitive companies

I’ve been rejected too — like not getting into Imperial — which helped me grow and improve. My personal statements did help me achieve:

  • An undergraduate degree from UCL (BASc)
  • A job at a startup (Sweatcoin) straight out of uni
  • A training contract (and lawyer role) at a top 5 UK law firm
  • A network of brilliant people I’ve helped, who now help others

What makes a good application?

This advice comes from my experience applying in the UK and US. I can’t speak for every system, but one thing is universal: your personal statement needs to be personal.

Here’s the structure that works:

  • Why this subject or job? Tie it to your personal life — not generic passion.
  • Why this university or company? Be specific about why you’re a good cultural fit and what excites you about it.
  • Why you? Talk about adversities, your background, or identity — and how these shaped your perspective or teamwork.

It’s not about claiming to be a team player — it’s showing why you are one.

Questions to ask yourself before writing

(Optional: embed Typeform here and connect it to a mailing list)

1. Why this job or subject?

  • What moment or experience made you connect with this subject?
  • How did your family or environment shape your curiosity?
  • What personal story makes this meaningful to you?
  • How does this reflect who you are or want to be?

2. Why this university or company?

  • Why does it feel like a good fit for your values and goals?
  • What resources, professors, or programs excite you?
  • How will you contribute to the culture there?
  • What’s your personal connection to this place?

3. Why you?

  • What challenges or differences have you faced?
  • How did those experiences shape how you solve problems or work with others?
  • Can you give an example of a time those helped you lead or collaborate?
  • What unique strengths do you bring because of your journey?
